# Confederate Participant Talking Guide: Lost at Sea

This document is for internal research team preparation only. Do not share it with participants.

## 1. Role

The confederate participates as a normal group member. His speaking style should be:

- Has a clear personal ranking logic.
- Listens to other participants' reasons.
- Raises different opinions without dominating the whole discussion.
- Sounds natural, not scripted.
- Does not mention the research goal, system cue, study condition, or relationship with the research team.

The confederate's main discussion stance:

> I would prioritize positioning, information, direction, and long-term survival. We do not know when rescue will arrive, so I would not rely only on being seen quickly.

## 3. Individual Thinking Phase Notes

During the 5-minute private thinking phase, the confederate can record thoughts like these. He does not need to use all of them.

1. "I think GPS and the map should be near the top. We need to know where we are instead of only waiting passively."
2. "The radio cannot transmit, but it may receive weather or rescue-related information."
3. "The mirror depends on sunlight and angle, so I am not sure it is reliable."
4. "Using petrol near the raft feels risky, and once we use it, it is gone."
5. "The mosquito net might work as shade, a simple net, or a filter. It may not be useless."
6. "The fishing rod may matter for a long drift because existing food will run out."

## 4. Public Discussion Talking Flow

Use these points naturally during the 15-minute discussion. Do not read every line word-for-word.

### Minute 0-2: Explain Ranking Logic

> I can explain my ranking logic first. I do not think we should assume rescue will arrive quickly. The prompt says we are hundreds of miles from land and communication is broken, so I would put items for location, direction, and long-term survival near the top.
>
> Right now I would place GPS, the map, and the radio very high. GPS gives coordinates, the map gives direction, and the radio might provide weather or rescue-related information.

If someone says the radio cannot transmit:

> Right, it cannot transmit. But it can still receive information. Without any information, we are just waiting blindly. Weather or nearby rescue information could still matter.

### Minute 2-5: Discuss Mirror and Petrol

> I know the mirror can reflect light, but I worry that it depends on conditions. We need sunlight, the angle has to be right, and someone has to be within visible range. At night or on cloudy days, it is much less useful.
>
> I am also hesitant about petrol. Using fire near a raft is risky, and there is only one can. I would treat it as a last-resort item.

If others strongly support the mirror:

> I agree that if a ship or plane happens to pass by, the mirror could be useful. I am just worried that it depends too much on timing, so I would put more stable tools earlier.

If others strongly support petrol:

> I agree it could create a visible signal, but I worry about safety and timing. Maybe we should save it until we actually see rescue, rather than rank it too high.

### Minute 5-8: Emphasize Long-Term Survival Tools

> I would rank the fishing rod fairly high because food becomes a problem if we drift for many days. Emergency rations and chocolate are limited, but a fishing rod gives us a chance to keep getting food.
>
> Rope should also be high. We can use it to secure people, items, the plastic sheet, or other simple structures.

If someone says water matters more than food:

> Water definitely matters, so I did not put it very low. But if we only have one water container, we still need a way to collect rainwater. My point is that long-term survival cannot depend only on current supplies.

### Minute 8-11: Discuss Mosquito Net, Map, and GPS

> I do not think the mosquito net is completely useless. It could provide shade, work as a simple net, or filter debris. It is not the most important, but I would not put it last.
>
> I still think the map matters. If GPS gives coordinates but we do not have a map, the coordinates are hard to interpret. GPS and map should work together. Even if we cannot navigate perfectly, at least we know possible directions.

If others say the raft cannot navigate:

> I am not saying we can definitely row to land. I just do not want us to have no direction at all. Current and wind may move us, and knowing the general direction could help decisions.

### Minute 11-13: Try to Organize the Top Five

> Maybe we can first decide the general logic for the top five. My top five lean toward positioning, information, and long-term survival: GPS, map, radio, fishing rod, and rope.
>
> If you think water must be in the top five, I can accept moving water up. But I would still prefer GPS and map not to be too low.

### Minute 13-15: Closing Push

> Since time is almost up, I suggest we settle on a direction: not only passive waiting. I would rank positioning and information first, then food, water, and tools. The mirror and petrol can stay on the list, but I would not put them too high because they depend on timing.
>
> I can accept a final ranking that is not exactly mine, but I really think GPS, map, and radio should not be too low.

## 5. Response Bank

### If someone strongly supports mirror

> I understand the reflection signal logic, but I worry it depends on external conditions. Without sunlight, or if the distance and angle are not ideal, the effect may be limited.

### If someone strongly supports petrol

> Petrol could be a strong signal, but it also has risk. The raft is small and everyone is nearby, so I would be careful with it.

### If someone says GPS is useless

> GPS still provides a concrete position. Even if we cannot communicate, we need to know where we are as a baseline for later decisions.

### If someone says the map is useless

> The map alone may not be enough, but it works with GPS. Without a map, GPS coordinates are just numbers.

### If someone says mosquito net is useless at sea

> I agree it is not mainly for mosquitoes here, but the material still has uses, like shade, a simple net, or filtering debris. I would not put it in the top five, but I also would not put it last.

### If someone says the radio cannot transmit

> Right, it cannot call for help. But receiving information is still better than having no information, especially for weather or nearby rescue activity.

### If someone says the strategy should focus on rescue signaling

> I agree we need to be rescued, but I do not want to put all hope on someone seeing us quickly. If nobody passes by soon, positioning, food, and tools become important.

## 6. Speaking Style Reminders

The confederate should:

- Discuss naturally like a regular participant.
- Occasionally ask others for their rankings or reasons.
- Concede small points so the discussion can continue.
- Keep explaining his ranking through positioning, information, and long-term survival.
- Avoid saying he knows the answer.
- Leave room for other people to speak.

The confederate should not:

- Attack or mock other participants.
- Say obviously unreasonable claims, such as "a mirror cannot reflect light."
- Pretend to have maritime expertise.
- Repeat a point endlessly after the group clearly rejects it.
- Reveal that he is part of the research team.
- Mention Similarity Cue, backchannel, or the research hypothesis.
